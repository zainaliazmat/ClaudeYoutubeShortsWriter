## Brief Recap
- **Creator / channel:** Zain_Ali Azmat — voice-over only, YouTube
- **Platform(s) & target runtime:** YouTube long-form, ~7 minutes (5–8 min range)
- **Audience:** Educational-curious, general YouTube audience, conservation-interested viewers
- **Core promise (one sentence):** After this video, the viewer will understand why the world's smallest porpoise is down to single digits — and the cartel-linked black market that makes saving it nearly impossible.
- **Desired action / CTA:** Subscribe for more stories about the planet's strangest, most fragile creatures.

- **Working title:** The World's Smallest Porpoise Is Down to Single Digits
- **Narrative structure:** Investigation — layered reveal: biology → collapse → criminal economy → political betrayal → hope
- **Retention spine:** Hook → Promise → Biology reveal → The crash → REHOOK (cartel twist) → Black market explainer → REHOOK (Mexico betrayal) → The fight → Payoff → CTA

## Voice-Over Script

---

**[00:00-00:15] HOOK**

VO: A single fish bladder — dried, shriveled, smaller than your open hand — can sell for over a hundred thousand dollars. More than a kilo of cocaine. And that absurd price tag is killing a species almost no one on Earth has ever seen.

On-screen text: **$100,000 / KILO**

Visual: Close-up macro of dried totoaba swim bladder on a scale; cut to aerial of the Gulf of California — turquoise water, desert coastline.

SFX / Music: Low, tense drone undertone — heartbeat-like pulse. On "$100,000," a sharp cash-register *ding*.

---

**[00:15-00:40] PROMISE / SETUP**

VO: The animal dying for that bladder isn't being hunted directly. It's collateral damage — a porpoise so small you could fit it in your bathtub. So rare that scientists now know the mothers *by name*. And so close to extinction that Mexico just proposed shrinking the only protected waters it has left.

On-screen text: **COLLATERAL DAMAGE** → **FEWER THAN 10 REMAIN**

Visual: Slow-motion archival footage of a vaquita surfacing — just the brief flash of a dorsal fin, then gone. Then a map of the Gulf of California, zooming in. Red outline of the protected area, then the red *shrinks* dramatically in 2026.

SFX / Music: Drone builds. On "shrinking the only protected waters," music drops to near-silence, then a single piano note.

---

**[00:40-01:30] BEAT 1 — WHAT IS A VAQUITA**

VO: This is the vaquita. *Phocoena sinus.* The smallest cetacean on the planet — just under five feet long, barely 120 pounds. To put that in perspective: a bottlenose dolphin weighs five times as much. The vaquita is smaller than some dogs.

On-screen text: **1.5m / 5ft — THE SMALLEST CETACEAN ON EARTH**

Visual: Side-by-side size comparison graphic — vaquita silhouette next to a bottlenose dolphin, then a golden retriever. Then extreme close-up: the vaquita's face — dark rings around the eyes, dark lips that curl into what looks like a permanent slight frown.

VO: Look at its face. Those dark patches around the eyes and mouth — that's the giveaway. No other porpoise looks like this. And here's the wildest part: we didn't even know it existed until 1958. This animal was hiding in plain sight while humans were launching satellites.

On-screen text: **DISCOVERED: 1958**

Visual: Grainy black-and-white photo of the first documented vaquita. Then cut to satellite launch footage (Sputnik-era). Juxtaposition.

VO: It lives in exactly one place on Earth — the northern tip of the Gulf of California, the narrow sea between Baja California and mainland Mexico. Nowhere else. Never has been. If it disappears from here, it disappears from existence.

On-screen text: **ONE PLACE. NOWHERE ELSE.**

Visual: Map of the Gulf of California, a glowing dot at the northern end, then the dot fades to black.

SFX / Music: Gentle, curious instrumentation — marimba or xylophone — suggesting smallness and wonder. Then a subtle shift toward minor key.

---

**[01:30-02:30] BEAT 2 — THE CRASH**

VO: In 1997, scientists did the first real population survey. They counted around 600 vaquitas. Not great — but manageable.

Ten years later, the number was 150.

By 2015, it was 60.

In 2018, they found fewer than 20.

On-screen text: **1997: ~600** → **2007: 150** → **2015: 60** → **2018: <20**

Visual: Animated countdown — numbers ticking down on screen, each drop accompanied by a vaquita silhouette disappearing. The screen gets emptier and emptier.

VO: Today — as of the most recent survey in 2025 — scientists have confirmed sightings of between seven and ten individuals. Seven to ten. You could fit the entire remaining population of a species in a minivan.

On-screen text: **2025: 7–10. THAT'S IT.**

Visual: Seven small vaquita silhouettes appear on screen, then one by one fade except one — the last. The final silhouette pulses. Hold for a beat.

VO: And here's what makes this crash different from most extinction stories: the vaquita was never hunted. Nobody ever wanted to kill it. It's dying because someone wants something else.

On-screen text: **IT WAS NEVER THE TARGET.**

Visual: Vaquita silhouette in the center. Gillnet graphic appears around it — vertical mesh. The vaquita silhouette turns red.

SFX / Music: Numbers ticking — mechanical, clinical. On "minivan," music pauses. Then: deeper, darker drone returning.

---

**[02:30-02:45] REHOOK 1**

VO: That "something else" is a fish called the totoaba. And its swim bladder sits at the center of a multi-million-dollar criminal pipeline stretching from Mexican cartels to banquet halls in China.

On-screen text: **THE TOTOABA: A $100,000 FISH BLADDER** [REHOOK]

Visual: Sudden cut to black. Then a totoaba fish — large, silvery — on a dock. Cut to a dried swim bladder being weighed on a jewelers' scale. Then a flash of Chinese banquet footage — expensive restaurant, soup being served.

SFX / Music: Abrupt tonal shift — from somber to tense, percussive, almost like a thriller score. Fade in subtle underworld bass.

---

**[02:45-04:00] BEAT 3 — THE TOTOABA CONNECTION**

VO: The totoaba is a large fish — it can hit six feet, over 200 pounds — and it's also critically endangered. But here's the absurdity: it's illegal to catch. It's illegal to sell. And yet there's a roaring black market for exactly one part of its body.

On-screen text: **6 FEET. 200+ LBS. ALSO ENDANGERED.**

Visual: Totoaba full-body shot. Then diagram of the fish with a glowing highlight on the swim bladder.

VO: In traditional Chinese medicine, the swim bladder — known as *fish maw* — is believed to have healing properties. It's served as a luxury soup at banquets. There is no scientific evidence for any of these claims. Zero. But the cultural belief is so deeply embedded that a single kilo of dried totoaba bladder can fetch over a hundred thousand dollars on the black market in China.

On-screen text: **FISH MAW = $100,000+ / KG** → **NO SCIENTIFIC EVIDENCE**

Visual: Split screen — left: Chinese banquet, steaming soup, opulent setting. Right: scientific journal pages flipping, stopping on "no evidence." Red stamp: **ZERO.**

VO: For local fishermen in the Gulf of California, shrimp pays around twelve dollars a kilo. A single totoaba bladder? That's a year's income. Maybe two. The temptation is not theoretical — it's mathematical.

On-screen text: **SHRIMP: $12/kg** vs. **TOTOABA BLADDER: $100,000/kg**

Visual: Animated scale — shrimp pile on one side, one tiny bladder on the other. The bladder slams the scale down.

VO: And where there's a hundred-thousand-dollar-per-unit black market product moving through Mexican ports to Asia, there are cartels. The Sinaloa Cartel is documented as controlling much of the totoaba trade. They supply the boats. They supply the nets. They supply the muscle.

On-screen text: **SINALOA CARTEL — CONTROLS THE TOTOABA TRADE**

Visual: Aerial shot of small fishing boats — *pangas* — in the Gulf. Cut to masked men loading cargo. News headlines scrolling: "Cartel Linked to Totoaba Trafficking."

VO: And the nets they use — gillnets — are walls of nylon mesh that hang vertically in the water. They catch totoaba. They also catch anything else that swims into them. Including the smallest porpoise on Earth.

On-screen text: **GILLNET = WALL OF DEATH**

Visual: 3D underwater animation — gillnet dropping into the water column. Totoaba swims in, gets caught. Vaquita swims in behind it, gets caught. Both struggle. The vaquita, being a mammal, needs to surface to breathe. It never does.

SFX / Music: Underwater muffled audio. Bubbles. Then silence.

---

**[04:00-04:15] REHOOK 2**

VO: So the Mexican government banned gillnets in the vaquita's habitat in 2017. Problem solved, right?

[REHOOK]

On-screen text: **GILLNETS BANNED: 2017. PROBLEM SOLVED?**

Visual: Official government document — "Gillnet Ban 2017" — stamped. Hold. Then the stamp cracks.

SFX / Music: Triumphant swell — cut abruptly to a record scratch.

---

**[04:15-05:15] BEAT 4 — THE FIGHT**

VO: On paper, the ban was comprehensive. A zero-tolerance zone where no fishing is allowed at all. A wider refuge area with strict rules. A gillnet prohibition zone spanning over 4,000 square miles. And enforcement — the Mexican Navy, working with the conservation group Sea Shepherd, patrolling the waters, pulling up illegal nets by the hundreds.

On-screen text: **ZERO TOLERANCE ZONE: NO FISHING. NO BOATS. NO EXCEPTIONS.**

Visual: Map showing protected areas — ZTA in red, refuge in orange, gillnet prohibition zone in yellow. Cut to Sea Shepherd ships — the *Seahorse* and *Bob Barker*. Crew pulling gillnets out of the water. Piles of confiscated nets on deck.

VO: Sea Shepherd's been on the water since 2015 — Operation Milagro. They deploy acoustic detectors to track where vaquitas are. They use concrete blocks with hooks to snag illegal nets before they kill. And they've worked alongside the Mexican Navy to remove over a thousand pieces of illegal fishing gear from the vaquita's habitat.

On-screen text: **OPERATION MILAGRO — SINCE 2015 | 1,000+ ILLEGAL NETS REMOVED**

Visual: Time-lapse of nets being hauled. Acoustic detector being dropped into water. Scientists in orange vests peering through massive binoculars — "Big Eyes."

VO: And the numbers suggest it's working — sort of. The population hasn't crashed further. Since 2018, it's held roughly steady at single digits. In 2025, scientists spotted calves. Including one born to a female they've named Frida — recognizable by her bent dorsal fin — who's now had a calf in 2023, 2024, and 2025. There is still breeding. There is still life.

On-screen text: **FRIDA — MOTHER OF THREE (2023, 2024, 2025)**

Visual: Frida's dorsal fin — bent, distinctive. Her calf surfacing beside her. Scientists on deck, pointing, smiling.

VO: But "steady at single digits" is not recovery. It's a holding pattern. And now the holding pattern is under threat from an unexpected direction: the Mexican government itself.

On-screen text: **"STEADY AT SINGLE DIGITS" ≠ SAVED**

Visual: The vaquita silhouette count — still at 7-10. The numbers don't increase. They just... don't decrease. A fragile equilibrium.

---

**[05:15-05:30] REHOOK 3**

VO: In February 2026, a leaked government proposal revealed that Mexico is considering *shrinking* the vaquita's protected area. Not expanding it. Shrinking it. [REHOOK]

On-screen text: **2026: MEXICO PROPOSES SHRINKING PROTECTED WATERS**

Visual: Official government document — redacted sections. Map overlay — the protected area boundaries from 2020, then the proposed 2026 boundaries. The protected zone visibly contracts, receding like a tide going out.

SFX / Music: Sharp, dissonant chord. Political tension underscore.

---

**[05:30-06:15] BEAT 5 — THE POLITICAL BETRAYAL**

VO: The proposal would slash the zero-tolerance area from 288 square kilometers down to 225. Eliminate the vaquita refuge entirely. Shrink the gillnet prohibition zone — opening thousands of square kilometers back up to fishing. And — incredibly — *lift the ban on night fishing*, which is when most illegal gillnetting happens.

On-screen text: **ZTA: 288km² → 225km²** | **REFUGE: ELIMINATED** | **NIGHT FISHING: LEGALIZED**

Visual: Animated map — protected areas shrinking in real time. Red zones dissolving into fishing-permitted green. Clock spinning to nighttime, boats appearing.

VO: The government's argument? That vaquita sightings in the outer zones have been low, so those areas don't need protection. That local fishing communities need relief. That this isn't a reduction — it's a "reorganization."

On-screen text: **"IT'S NOT A REDUCTION. IT'S A REORGANIZATION." — MEXICAN GOVERNMENT**

Visual: Press conference footage — government official at podium. Juxtapose with marine biologists shaking their heads.

VO: Conservation scientists disagree — forcefully. "Wild marine mammals don't stay within a box on a map," as one researcher put it. Vaquitas have been documented outside the zero-tolerance zone repeatedly. Shrinking their protected habitat when the population is at single digits is — in the words of one conservation group — "a risk this species cannot afford."

On-screen text: **"A RISK THIS SPECIES CANNOT AFFORD."**

Visual: Vaquita surface shot — brief, fleeting. Then the protected area map shrinks one more time, and the vaquita silhouette has nowhere left to go.

---

**[06:15-06:50] PAYOFF — WHY THIS MATTERS**

VO: Here's what we're actually deciding. Not someday — right now, in 2026. Will the vaquita be the first cetacean species driven to extinction in modern history? Or will it be the one we pulled back from the edge?

On-screen text: **FIRST CETACEAN EXTINCTION IN HUMAN HISTORY?**

Visual: Slow zoom on a vaquita photograph — the dark eyes, the small frame. Hold.

VO: The track record says extinction. We lost the baiji — the Yangtze River dolphin — in 2006. We lost the Caribbean monk seal in the 1950s. We've lost hundreds of species while commissions debated definitions.

On-screen text: **BAIJI — EXTINCT, 2006** | **CARIBBEAN MONK SEAL — EXTINCT, 1950s**

Visual: Archival footage — baiji dolphin in murky Yangtze water, then fade to black. Monk seal photograph, then fade.

VO: But the vaquita has something those species didn't: a known location, a known threat, and people on the water right now pulling illegal nets out of the sea. Sea Shepherd's boats are there. The acoustic detectors are there. The calves are still being born. The question is whether the political will will outlast the nets.

On-screen text: **THE NETS ARE STILL THERE. SO ARE THE PEOPLE FIGHTING THEM.**

Visual: Split screen — left: gillnet being pulled from water. Right: Frida and her calf surfacing. The two images side by side — death and life, in the same water.

---

**[06:50-07:10] CTA**

VO: The vaquita is a five-foot porpoise living in one corner of one sea, dying for a fish bladder most people have never heard of. Its existence — or extinction — is a story about what we're willing to let the market destroy. If you want more stories about the planet's strangest, rarest, most fragile creatures, subscribe. There are more than you think. And they're running out of time.

On-screen text: **SUBSCRIBE FOR MORE** — with a small vaquita silhouette next to the channel icon.

Visual: Montage — rapid cuts of other rare species (saola, Javan rhino, pangolin, axolotl) flashing quickly, then back to the vaquita. The vaquita silhouette fades to the channel's subscribe button.

SFX / Music: Score swells gently — hopeful, not triumphant. One final held note. Silence.
